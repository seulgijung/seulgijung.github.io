On February 6, 2024, the Korean Constitutional Court, equivalent to the U.S. Supreme Court, ruled that the fetus sex disclosure ban was unconstitutional, a landmark ruling that overturned a 37-year-old practice.

Before the total abolition, obstetrician-gynecologists were permitted to inform their clients of the baby's sex only after 32 weeks of pregnancy. But this right of access to the baby's sex was earned through a ruling on a constitutional complaint in 2009, over a decade and a half ago.

Does that mean it was impossible before 2009 to know the baby's sex before birth? History says otherwise.

Ultrasound machines were first introduced to Seoul National University Hospital and Chung-Ang University Hospital in 1978 and were not widely distributed to local clinics until the first Korean-made device was successfully produced in 1983. The state-of-the-art medical devices were revolutionary: they enabled medical professionals to conduct routine antenatal care, such as monitoring fetal development and heart activity, reassuring parents that their newborns-to-be were growing adequately and healthily.

This joyful breakthrough, however, brought displeasure and disappointment to some, especially to baby girls' parents. In a strongly male-oriented society where girls were devalued, the technology was quickly employed to confirm the baby's sex, and some parents chose to abort the baby girl.

What fueled the killing of baby girls was superstitious beliefs in Chinese zodiac signs that accord with the lunar calendar. Associated with the tiger, dragon, and horse, positive personal traits — brave, tenacious, and ambitious — for boys were believed to be negatively translated for girls — arrogant, stubborn, and haughty — and prospective parents avoided giving birth to girls in the years of the tiger, dragon, and horse as a result. As the chart illustrates, while parental discrimination against girls abated over time, the 1997 law outlawing fetal sex determination could not prevent those years from being smeared in blood or from seeing a conspicuous spike in both cycles between the late '80s and early '00s.

This zodiac sign fuss over birth is well documented. A Seoul Economic Daily article from December 1998 — the year of the tiger — paints a picture of local OBGYN clinics bombarded with inquiries about the possibility of deliberately delayed delivery so that their girls could be born in the year of the rabbit, a creature regarded as docile and amenable. "Nevertheless boys born in the year of horse, dragon or tiger are regarded as courageous and manly, girls are predisposed to be tough, impatient, and reckless," the article reads.

This massacre was not carried out uniformly in terms of geography. What drove the girl-targeted manslaughter on a fundamental level lay in neo-Confucian traditions that are still entrenched in Korean society. Divergent from ancient Chinese Confucianism, neo-Confucianism elevated Confucian ethical teachings to cosmological principle, as though it were as natural for men to hold power over women as water flowing downward.

Hugely popularized during the Joseon dynasty, the first neo-Confucian institutes nestled in Gyeongsang Province, and as of 1864 a majority of such schools were located in the region. These educational organizations were central to reproducing neo-Confucian norms and regional aristocratic society.

The analysis on SRB for third child and beyond gives us a window into families whose family planning stopped after giving birth to a son, the so-called "stopping rule," along with selective abortions. In this, Gyeongsang areas disproportionately crowded the early ranks in 1990, taking four spots out of the top five, with Daejeon as the only non-Gyeongsang city.

The analysis of average total birth sex ratio from 1997 through 2005 shows that the trend had been heavily spearheaded by Gyeongsang Province, with all five of its regions, including metropolitan cities like Daegu, topping the list.
